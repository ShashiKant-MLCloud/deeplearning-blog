Part 1

Activation Functions Beyond Sigmoid

In Part 1 of this series we used Sigmoid as the activation function throughout the entire network. This is mathematically clean for teaching but problematic in practice. Before introducing alternatives, we need to understand precisely why Sigmoid fails in hidden layers.

Why Sigmoid Fails in Hidden Layers

Recall the sigmoid derivative we proved in Part 1:

$$\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1 - \sigma(z))$$

Its maximum value occurs at $z = 0$:

$$\sigma'(0) = 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25$$

For any other input, the derivative is strictly less than 0.25. In a deep network with $L$ hidden layers, the gradient flowing back to the earliest layer is proportional to:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} \propto \prod_{l=1}^{L} \sigma'(Z^l) \leq (0.25)^L$$
LayersMax gradient (best case)Practical reality
1$0.25^1 = 0.250$Acceptable
3$0.25^3 = 0.016$Weak
5$0.25^5 = 0.001$Near zero
10$0.25^{10} \approx 10^{-6}$Effectively dead
The Vanishing Gradient Problem: Early layers receive gradients so close to zero that their weights barely update. The network's ability to learn complex representations — which depends on early layers — is severely compromised. This is why deep networks with sigmoid activations were largely untrainable before 2010.

Each alternative activation below addresses this problem in a different way.

Tanh — Hyperbolic Tangent

Definition

$$\tanh(z) = \frac{e^z - e^{-z}}{e^z + e^{-z}}$$

Equivalently, tanh is a scaled and shifted version of sigmoid:

$$\tanh(z) = 2\sigma(2z) - 1$$

Derivative of Tanh — Full Proof

Step 1 Write as a quotient $f/g$ where $f = e^z - e^{-z}$ and $g = e^z + e^{-z}$:

$$\tanh'(z) = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$$

Step 2 Compute each part:

$$f' = e^z + e^{-z} = g \quad\quad g' = e^z - e^{-z} = f$$

Step 3 Substitute:

$$\tanh'(z) = \frac{g \cdot g - f \cdot f}{g^2} = \frac{g^2 - f^2}{g^2} = 1 - \frac{f^2}{g^2} = 1 - \tanh^2(z)$$
$$\tanh'(z) = 1 - \tanh^2(z)$$

Like sigmoid, the derivative is expressed in terms of the function itself — whatever was computed in the forward pass is reused. The maximum derivative occurs at $z = 0$:

$$\tanh'(0) = 1 - 0^2 = 1$$

This is 4 times larger than sigmoid's maximum of 0.25. The vanishing gradient problem is reduced but not eliminated — tanh still saturates at $\pm 1$ for large $|z|$.

Zero-centered advantage: Tanh outputs values in $(-1, +1)$, centered around zero. Sigmoid outputs in $(0, 1)$, always positive. When all activations are positive, weight gradients in the same layer always have the same sign, forcing zig-zag updates that slow convergence. Tanh's zero-centering avoids this.

ReLU — Rectified Linear Unit

Definition

$$\text{ReLU}(z) = \max(0, z) = \begin{cases} z & z > 0 \\ 0 & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

Derivative

$$\text{ReLU}'(z) = \begin{cases} 1 & z > 0 \\ 0 & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

The derivative at exactly $z = 0$ is technically undefined (the function has a kink), but in practice is set to either 0 or 1 — this edge case occurs with probability zero during training and has no practical impact.

Why ReLU Solves the Vanishing Gradient

For any active neuron ($z > 0$), the derivative is exactly 1. The gradient product across $L$ active layers becomes:

$$\prod_{l=1}^{L} \text{ReLU}'(Z^l) = 1 \times 1 \times \cdots \times 1 = 1$$

The gradient passes through active neurons completely unchanged in magnitude, regardless of network depth. This is what made training very deep networks practical.

Advantages

Gradient = 1 for active neurons — no vanishing. Computationally trivial: just $\max(0, z)$. Sparse activations (~50% neurons inactive) act as natural regularization.

Dying ReLU Problem

If $z \leq 0$ for all training samples — caused by a large negative bias or bad weight update — gradient = 0 permanently. The neuron never recovers. A large learning rate can kill many neurons simultaneously.

Leaky ReLU

Definition

$$\text{LeakyReLU}(z) = \begin{cases} z & z > 0 \\ \alpha z & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

where $\alpha$ is a small constant, typically $\alpha = 0.01$.

Derivative

$$\text{LeakyReLU}'(z) = \begin{cases} 1 & z > 0 \\ \alpha & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

The derivative for negative inputs is now $\alpha = 0.01$ instead of 0. This small but non-zero gradient allows neurons that fall into the negative zone to still receive a gradient signal and potentially recover.

Why $\alpha = 0.01$ specifically? It is small enough that negative-zone neurons do not dominate the gradient (they shouldn't — they were inactive for a reason), but large enough to prevent permanent death. The exact value is a hyperparameter; some implementations learn it during training (Parametric ReLU).

ELU — Exponential Linear Unit

Definition

$$\text{ELU}(z) = \begin{cases} z & z > 0 \\ \alpha(e^z - 1) & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

where $\alpha > 0$, typically $\alpha = 1.0$.

Derivative

$$\text{ELU}'(z) = \begin{cases} 1 & z > 0 \\ \alpha e^z & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

Note that for $z \leq 0$: $\text{ELU}(z) + \alpha = \alpha e^z$, so the derivative can also be written as $\text{ELU}(z) + \alpha$ — reusing the forward pass value, just like sigmoid and tanh.

Key Properties

Smooth at zero. Unlike ReLU and Leaky ReLU which have a sharp kink at $z = 0$, ELU is differentiable everywhere. At $z = 0$: $\text{ELU}(0) = \alpha(e^0 - 1) = 0$ (continuous) and $\text{ELU}'(0^-) = \alpha e^0 = \alpha = 1$ when $\alpha = 1$ (smooth).

Negative saturation. As $z \to -\infty$, $\text{ELU}(z) \to -\alpha$. Mean activations are pushed closer to zero, which stabilizes training similarly to batch normalization.

GELU — Gaussian Error Linear Unit

Definition

$$\text{GELU}(z) = z \cdot \Phi(z) = z \cdot \frac{1}{2}\left[1 + \text{erf}\left(\frac{z}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\right]$$

where $\Phi(z)$ is the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the standard normal distribution, and $\text{erf}$ is the Gaussian error function.

Practical Approximation

The exact form involves the error function which is expensive to compute. In practice, the following approximation is used:

$$\text{GELU}(z) \approx 0.5z\left(1 + \tanh\left[\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\left(z + 0.044715z^3\right)\right]\right)$$

What Does It Mean Probabilistically?

$\text{GELU}(z) = z \cdot \Phi(z)$ can be read as: the input $z$ is multiplied by the probability that a standard normal random variable is less than $z$. Intuitively, inputs that are very likely to be above zero (large positive $z$, $\Phi(z) \approx 1$) pass through fully. Inputs unlikely to be above zero (large negative $z$, $\Phi(z) \approx 0$) are suppressed. This is a smooth, probabilistic gating.

Where GELU is used: GPT-2, GPT-3, BERT, and most modern transformer architectures use GELU in their feed-forward layers. It empirically outperforms ReLU on these architectures, likely because its smooth non-linearity is better suited to the attention-based representations these networks learn.

Part 2

Loss Functions Beyond MSE

In Part 1, we used Mean Squared Error (MSE) as the loss function and derived its gradient fully. MSE is the natural choice for regression — predicting continuous values. But applying it to classification problems creates two serious issues.

Why MSE Fails for Classification

Problem 1 — MSE punishes errors geometrically, not probabilistically. When the output is a probability $\hat{y} \in (0,1)$, the squared difference $(y - \hat{y})^2$ treats all equal-distance errors equally. Being wrong with high confidence ($\hat{y} = 0.01$ when $y = 1$) should be punished catastrophically. MSE only penalizes it by $(1 - 0.01)^2 = 0.980$. Being mildly uncertain ($\hat{y} = 0.4$ when $y = 1$) is penalized $(1 - 0.4)^2 = 0.360$. The confident wrong prediction gets only 2.7× more penalty despite being far more consequential.

Problem 2 — MSE reintroduces vanishing gradients at the output. Recall from Part 1, the output gradient with MSE + sigmoid:

$$\delta^2 = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot \hat{y}(1 - \hat{y})$$

When the network is very confident but wrong — say $\hat{y} = 0.01$, $y = 1$:

$$\delta^2 = (0.01 - 1) \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.99 = -0.99 \times 0.0099 = -0.0098$$

The error signal $(\hat{y} - y) = -0.99$ is large, but the sigmoid derivative $\hat{y}(1-\hat{y}) = 0.0099$ kills it. The network barely corrects its worst mistakes — precisely the behavior we do not want.

Information Theory Foundation

Cross-entropy loss emerges naturally from information theory, not from arbitrary design choices.

Entropy measures the average surprise or uncertainty in a probability distribution $p$:

$$H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \log(p_i)$$

The term $-\log(p_i)$ is the information content of event $i$ — rare events ($p_i \approx 0$) carry high information ($-\log(p_i) \to \infty$); certain events carry no information ($-\log(1) = 0$).

Cross-entropy measures how well a predicted distribution $\hat{y}$ approximates the true distribution $y$:

$$H(y, \hat{y}) = -\sum_i y_i \log(\hat{y}_i)$$
Minimizing cross-entropy is equivalent to finding the predicted distribution $\hat{y}$ that most closely matches the true distribution $y$ — which is the correct objective for any classification task. MSE has no such probabilistic grounding.

Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE)

Used when: binary classification — two classes, one output neuron, sigmoid activation.

Definition

For a single training example with true label $y \in \{0, 1\}$ and prediction $\hat{y} \in (0, 1)$:

$$L_{BCE} = -\left[y\log(\hat{y}) + (1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})\right]$$

Understanding the Two Terms

This is two cases collapsed into one equation using the indicator $y \in \{0,1\}$.

When $y = 1$: $(1-y) = 0$, so the second term vanishes:

$$L = -\log(\hat{y})$$

As $\hat{y} \to 1$ (correct, confident): $-\log(1) = 0$ — no loss. As $\hat{y} \to 0$ (wrong, confident): $-\log(0) \to \infty$ — catastrophic loss.

When $y = 0$: $y = 0$, so the first term vanishes:

$$L = -\log(1-\hat{y})$$

As $\hat{y} \to 0$ (correct, confident): $-\log(1) = 0$. As $\hat{y} \to 1$ (wrong, confident): $-\log(0) \to \infty$.

BCE punishes confident wrong predictions exponentially harder — this is the correct behavior for probabilistic outputs.

BCE Gradient & The Magic Cancellation

Step 1 — Gradient of BCE w.r.t. ŷ

$$\frac{\partial L_{BCE}}{\partial \hat{y}} = \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{y}}\left[-y\log(\hat{y}) - (1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})\right]$$

Differentiating term by term using $\frac{d}{dx}\log(x) = \frac{1}{x}$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{y}}\left[-y\log(\hat{y})\right] = -\frac{y}{\hat{y}}$$ $$\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{y}}\left[-(1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})\right] = \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}}$$

Combining over a common denominator $\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$:

$$\frac{\partial L_{BCE}}{\partial \hat{y}} = -\frac{y}{\hat{y}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}} = \frac{-y(1-\hat{y}) + (1-y)\hat{y}}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})} = \frac{\hat{y} - y}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})}$$
$$\frac{\partial L_{BCE}}{\partial \hat{y}} = \frac{\hat{y} - y}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})}$$

Step 2 — Apply the Chain Rule to Get δ²

Since $\hat{y} = \sigma(Z^2)$, the output layer error signal is:

$$\delta^2 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial Z^2} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial Z^2} = \frac{\hat{y} - y}{\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})} \cdot \hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$$
$$\delta^2 = \hat{y} - y$$

The $\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$ term — the sigmoid derivative — appears in the denominator of $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}}$ and cancels exactly with the sigmoid derivative from the chain rule. The gradient is always cleanly proportional to the prediction error, never vanishing regardless of how confident the network is.

BCE + Sigmoid (Correct)

Matched Pairs — Activation + Loss

The most important design insight: output activation and loss function must always be chosen as a matched pair. Each pairing is specifically designed so their combined gradient is clean and the sigmoid/softmax derivative cancels out.

RegressionLinear (none)MSE$(\hat{y}-y)$
Binary classificationSigmoidBinary Cross-Entropy$(\hat{y}-y)$

All three correctly-matched pairs produce the identical gradient form $\delta^2 = \hat{y} - y$. This is not a coincidence — the activation function and loss function are engineered together so their derivatives cancel, guaranteeing a clean error signal at the very first step of backpropagation. Everything upstream inherits this clean signal.
Part 3

Softmax Output Layer

Binary Cross-Entropy handles two classes. When there are $N > 2$ mutually exclusive classes, we need a generalization. Softmax converts a vector of raw scores into a proper probability distribution over $N$ classes.

Definition & Properties

For an output layer with $N$ neurons, the pre-activation vector is $\mathbf{Z^2} = [z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_N]^T$. Softmax maps this to a probability vector:

$$\hat{y}_i = \text{softmax}(z_i) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{N} e^{z_k}}, \quad i = 1, 2, \ldots, N$$

Three Essential Properties

Property 1 — All outputs are positive: Since $e^{z_i} > 0$ for all real $z_i$, and the denominator is a sum of positive numbers, $\hat{y}_i > 0$ always.

Property 2 — Outputs sum to 1:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{N} \hat{y}_i = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}} = \frac{\sum_i e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}} = 1$$

Together, Properties 1 and 2 make $\hat{y}$ a valid probability distribution.

Property 3 — Translation invariance: Adding a constant $c$ to all inputs leaves the output unchanged:

$$\frac{e^{z_i + c}}{\sum_k e^{z_k + c}} = \frac{e^c \cdot e^{z_i}}{e^c \cdot \sum_k e^{z_k}} = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}} = \hat{y}_i$$
Numerical stability trick: In practice, $e^{z_i}$ can overflow to infinity for large $z_i$. By Property 3, setting $c = -\max_k(z_k)$ shifts all inputs to be $\leq 0$ before exponentiation, preventing overflow without changing the output. This is standard in all implementations.

The Softmax Jacobian — Why It Is Not Diagonal

Recall that for the sigmoid hidden layer in Part 1, the Jacobian $\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}$ was diagonal because each neuron's activation depended only on its own input. Softmax is fundamentally different — every output $\hat{y}_i$ depends on all inputs $z_k$ via the shared denominator $\sum_k e^{z_k}$. The Jacobian is fully dense.

Case 1: $i = k$ (Diagonal Elements)

$$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z_i}\left[\frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}}\right]$$

Apply quotient rule with $f = e^{z_i}$, $g = \sum_k e^{z_k}$, so $f' = e^{z_i}$, $g' = e^{z_i}$:

$$= \frac{e^{z_i} \cdot \sum_k e^{z_k} - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_i}}{\left(\sum_k e^{z_k}\right)^2} = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}} - \left(\frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}}\right)^2 = \hat{y}_i - \hat{y}_i^2$$
$$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_i} = \hat{y}_i(1 - \hat{y}_i) \quad \text{(diagonal)}$$

Case 2: $i \neq k$ (Off-Diagonal Elements)

$$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z_k}\left[\frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}\right]$$

Now $e^{z_i}$ is constant w.r.t. $z_k$ (since $i \neq k$), so only the denominator changes with $g' = e^{z_k}$:

$$= \frac{0 \cdot \sum_j e^{z_j} - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_k}}{\left(\sum_j e^{z_j}\right)^2} = -\frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} \cdot \frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$$
$$\frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k} = -\hat{y}_i \cdot \hat{y}_k \quad \text{(off-diagonal)}$$

The Full Softmax Jacobian

$$\frac{\partial \hat{\mathbf{y}}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^2}} = \begin{bmatrix} \hat{y}_1(1-\hat{y}_1) & -\hat{y}_1\hat{y}_2 & \cdots & -\hat{y}_1\hat{y}_N \\ -\hat{y}_2\hat{y}_1 & \hat{y}_2(1-\hat{y}_2) & \cdots & -\hat{y}_2\hat{y}_N \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ -\hat{y}_N\hat{y}_1 & -\hat{y}_N\hat{y}_2 & \cdots & \hat{y}_N(1-\hat{y}_N) \end{bmatrix}$$
Critical difference from the hidden layer Jacobian: The sigmoid hidden layer Jacobian was diagonal because neurons were independent. Softmax's Jacobian is fully dense because all outputs share the same denominator — changing any one $z_k$ redistributes probability mass across all outputs simultaneously. This makes the gradient derivation below significantly more involved.

Categorical Cross-Entropy (CCE)

For multi-class classification with $N$ classes, the true label is encoded as a one-hot vector — all zeros except a single 1 at the correct class index $c$:

$$y = [0, \ldots, 0, \underbrace{1}_{c}, 0, \ldots, 0]$$

The loss is:

$$L_{CCE} = -\sum_{i=1}^{N} y_i \log(\hat{y}_i)$$

Because $y$ is one-hot, all terms with $y_i = 0$ vanish. Only the correct class $c$ survives:

$$L_{CCE} = -\log(\hat{y}_c)$$

This is beautifully simple: the entire loss reduces to the negative log of the probability assigned to the correct class. Examples:

CCE + Softmax Gradient — Full Derivation

We need $\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k}$ for all $k = 1, \ldots, N$ — the gradient of CCE w.r.t. each output pre-activation.

Step 1 — Gradient of CCE w.r.t. ŷᵢ

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}_i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{y}_i}\left[-\sum_j y_j \log \hat{y}_j\right] = -\frac{y_i}{\hat{y}_i}$$

Step 2 — Apply the Matrix Chain Rule

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(-\frac{y_i}{\hat{y}_i}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}_i}{\partial z_k}$$

Step 3 — Split into Diagonal and Off-Diagonal

Using the two Jacobian cases derived above:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = \left(-\frac{y_k}{\hat{y}_k}\right)\cdot\hat{y}_k(1-\hat{y}_k) + \sum_{i \neq k}\left(-\frac{y_i}{\hat{y}_i}\right)\cdot(-\hat{y}_i\hat{y}_k)$$

Step 4 — Simplify

$$= -y_k(1-\hat{y}_k) + \sum_{i \neq k} y_i \hat{y}_k$$ $$= -y_k + y_k\hat{y}_k + \hat{y}_k\sum_{i \neq k} y_i$$ $$= -y_k + \hat{y}_k\left[y_k + \sum_{i \neq k} y_i\right]$$ $$= -y_k + \hat{y}_k \cdot \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{N} y_i}_{= 1 \text{ since y is one-hot}}$$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = \hat{y}_k - y_k$$

Despite the fully dense Softmax Jacobian — every entry non-zero, far more complex than the diagonal Jacobian in Part 1 — the entire computation collapses to the same clean form: prediction minus truth. The one-hot property ($\sum_i y_i = 1$) is the key that triggers this cancellation.

In vector form: $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^2}} = \hat{\mathbf{y}} - \mathbf{y}$. Each output neuron's gradient is simply its predicted probability minus its true label (0 or 1). For the correct class: gradient $= \hat{y}_c - 1$ (negative, pushing probability up). For all other classes: gradient $= \hat{y}_k - 0 = \hat{y}_k$ (positive, pushing probability down). This is exactly the correct corrective behavior.

Numerical Example — 3-Class Classification

Setup

Classes:  Cat (0), Dog (1), Bird (2)
True label: Dog  →  y = [0, 1, 0]  (one-hot)
Output pre-activations: Z² = [2.0, 4.0, 1.5]

Forward Pass — Softmax

Compute $e^{z_i}$ for each class (using numerical stability: subtract max = 4.0):

$$z' = [2.0-4.0,\ 4.0-4.0,\ 1.5-4.0] = [-2.0,\ 0.0,\ -2.5]$$ $$e^{z'} = [e^{-2.0},\ e^{0},\ e^{-2.5}] = [0.1353,\ 1.0,\ 0.0821]$$ $$\text{Sum} = 0.1353 + 1.0 + 0.0821 = 1.2174$$ $$\hat{y} = \left[\frac{0.1353}{1.2174},\ \frac{1.0}{1.2174},\ \frac{0.0821}{1.2174}\right] = [0.111,\ 0.822,\ 0.067]$$

Loss

$$L_{CCE} = -\log(\hat{y}_{\text{Dog}}) = -\log(0.822) = 0.196$$

The network correctly assigns 82.2% probability to Dog — loss is low.

Gradient

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^2}} = \hat{\mathbf{y}} - \mathbf{y} = [0.111 - 0,\ 0.822 - 1,\ 0.067 - 0] = [0.111,\ -0.178,\ 0.067]$$

Interpretation:

  • Cat: gradient $= +0.111$ → push $z_{\text{Cat}}$ down, reducing Cat's probability
  • Dog: gradient $= -0.178$ → push $z_{\text{Dog}}$ up, increasing Dog's probability
  • Bird: gradient $= +0.067$ → push $z_{\text{Bird}}$ down, reducing Bird's probability

The gradient naturally corrects all three outputs simultaneously in the right direction.

Part 4

The Unified Pattern

Stepping back across all three parts of this series, one pattern emerges with remarkable consistency.

Every correctly matched pair of output activation and loss function produces the same gradient form at the output layer:

$$\boxed{\delta^2 = \hat{y} - y}$$

This holds for MSE + linear output (regression), BCE + sigmoid (binary classification), and CCE + softmax (multi-class). The derivations are completely different — MSE involves a squared difference, BCE involves logarithms and information theory, CCE involves a dense Jacobian — yet all three arrive at the same destination.

This is not accidental. Each pairing is an engineered matching where the activation function's derivative and the loss function's gradient are specifically designed to cancel. The result is that backpropagation always begins with the cleanest possible signal: prediction minus truth, unobscured by any additional factors.

For hidden layers, the pattern is different — the sigmoid derivative $A(1-A)$ does appear and does not cancel, which is exactly why we replace sigmoid with ReLU (derivative = 1) or its variants. The vanishing gradient problem is a hidden-layer problem; the clean gradient design is an output-layer solution. Understanding both sides of this — why outputs are designed one way and hidden layers another — is the core of understanding neural network architecture decisions.

Part 5

Weight Initialization

Everything we have discussed so far assumes the network already has weights to update. But every network must start somewhere — weights must be assigned before the first forward pass. The choice of those initial values turns out to matter enormously, and getting it wrong can make training fail completely before a single gradient update is applied.

Why Initialization Matters

Think of initialization as setting the starting conditions for the entire learning process. The gradients we computed in Parts 1 and 2 flow backward through the network and update weights from wherever they started. If the starting point is poor, the network may never recover — not because the learning algorithm is broken, but because the initial signal is already broken.

There are two ways initialization can go wrong, and they are mirror images of each other.

Too large — Exploding activations and gradients. If initial weights are large, the pre-activation values $Z = WX + b$ become large. For sigmoid and tanh, large inputs cause saturation — the neuron output is stuck near 0 or 1, the gradient is near zero, and learning stops immediately. For ReLU, large weights can cause gradients to explode as they flow backward through layers, making weight updates wildly unstable.

Too small — Vanishing activations and gradients. If initial weights are very small (say, all near zero), then every layer's output is also near zero regardless of input. The network collapses — all neurons produce essentially the same output, and there is no meaningful gradient signal to differentiate them. Worse, all neurons evolve identically during training because they started identically. This is called the symmetry problem.

Never initialize all weights to zero. If every weight is zero, every neuron in a layer computes the exact same output, receives the exact same gradient, and updates to the exact same value. No matter how long you train, all neurons in a layer remain identical — the network has effectively just one neuron per layer. Weights must be initialized asymmetrically, which is why random initialization is always used.

Initialization Options

Random Uniform / Random Normal (Naive)

The simplest approach: draw each weight from a uniform distribution (e.g. between −0.05 and +0.05) or a normal distribution with a small standard deviation. This breaks symmetry, which is the minimum requirement. However, it ignores the network's depth and width, which means it tends to produce either too-large or too-small activations as the network grows deeper. Fine for very shallow networks; problematic otherwise.

Xavier / Glorot Initialization

Proposed by Xavier Glorot and Yoshua Bengio in 2010, this is the standard choice for networks using sigmoid or tanh activations. The core idea is elegant: keep the variance of activations approximately equal across all layers, in both the forward and backward pass.

If the variance shrinks layer by layer (small weights), signals vanish. If it grows (large weights), signals explode. Xavier initialization sets the initial weight variance to balance these two pressures simultaneously, based on the number of inputs ($n_{in}$) and outputs ($n_{out}$) of each layer.

Intuitively: a layer with many input connections needs smaller individual weights so that the sum does not explode. Xavier scales the weights accordingly — wider layers get smaller initial weights.

Key assumption: Xavier initialization is derived assuming the activation function is approximately linear near zero — which holds for sigmoid and tanh (both behave like a line near $z = 0$). This assumption breaks down for ReLU, which is why a different initialization is needed for ReLU networks.

He / Kaiming Initialization

Proposed by Kaiming He in 2015, specifically designed for ReLU and its variants. The derivation follows the same logic as Xavier — keep variance stable across layers — but accounts for the fact that ReLU zeroes out half of all inputs (the negative half). Since half the neurons are inactive, only half the weights are effectively contributing to the signal at any moment. He initialization compensates by making the initial weights roughly $\sqrt{2}$ times larger than Xavier would suggest, restoring the signal variance that ReLU's zeroing would otherwise destroy.

This is now the default initialization in most deep learning frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow) when using ReLU activations.

Orthogonal Initialization

Rather than sampling weights randomly from a distribution, orthogonal initialization constructs the weight matrix to be an orthogonal matrix — one where the rows and columns are all perpendicular to each other and of equal length. The practical effect is that the network's initial transformation neither stretches nor compresses the signal in any particular direction. This is particularly useful for very deep networks and recurrent networks where the same weight matrix is applied repeatedly across time steps.

Pre-trained / Transfer Learning Initialization

Instead of starting from random weights, initialize the network with weights from a model already trained on a related task. For example, initialize an image classifier with weights from a model trained on ImageNet. Only the final output layer is randomized. This is called transfer learning and is arguably the most powerful initialization strategy in practice — the network starts not from random noise but from learned, meaningful representations. Fine-tuning then adjusts these weights for the specific task at hand.

Part 6

Optimizers Beyond Gradient Descent

The basic gradient descent update rule we proved in Part 1 is:

$$W := W - \alpha \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial W}$$

This works in principle but has practical limitations that become severe as networks scale. All modern optimizers are improvements built on top of this foundation. Understanding what each one fixes — and why — is essential for knowing which to choose.

The Problems With Vanilla Gradient Descent

Problem 1 — Same learning rate for every parameter. Vanilla gradient descent applies the same $\alpha$ to every single weight in the network, regardless of how frequently it is updated, how large its gradients are, or how sensitive the loss is to it. In practice, different parameters need very different effective learning rates. A weight connected to a rare input feature should update aggressively when that feature appears; a weight updated constantly should be more conservative.

Problem 2 — Oscillation in ravines. Loss landscapes often have long narrow valleys — steep in one direction, shallow in another (imagine a stretched bowl). Gradient descent with a fixed learning rate oscillates wildly across the steep dimension while barely moving along the shallow dimension toward the minimum. Progress is slow and erratic.

Problem 3 — Getting stuck in local minima and saddle points. A pure gradient-following algorithm has no mechanism to escape a local minimum or push through a saddle point where the gradient is zero in some directions. It simply stops.

Each optimizer below addresses one or more of these problems.

Momentum

Momentum addresses the oscillation problem by introducing a velocity term — a memory of recent gradient directions. Instead of taking a step purely based on the current gradient, the update accumulates a running average of past gradients.

Think of it physically: a ball rolling down a hill does not just respond to the current slope — it has built up speed in a consistent direction. The ball accelerates through flat regions, smoothly curves around obstacles, and dampens oscillations because its momentum carries it through brief reversals.

In practice: if the gradients have been consistently pointing in the same direction across many updates, the velocity grows and the effective step size increases — the network accelerates toward the minimum. If gradients keep reversing direction (oscillation), the opposing velocities cancel each other and the step size shrinks — oscillation is damped.

The momentum coefficient (typically 0.9) controls how much history is retained — a value of 0.9 means 90% of the previous velocity is carried forward.

Nesterov Momentum is a refinement: instead of computing the gradient at the current position and then applying momentum, it first takes a step in the momentum direction and then computes the gradient. This "look-ahead" behavior corrects the trajectory before committing to the full update, leading to faster convergence on many problems.

AdaGrad — Adaptive Gradient

AdaGrad addresses the same-learning-rate problem by adapting the learning rate individually for each parameter, based on its history of gradients. The core idea: parameters that have received large gradients historically should get a smaller learning rate; parameters that have received small gradients should get a larger learning rate.

It does this by accumulating the sum of squared gradients for each parameter and dividing the learning rate by the square root of that sum. A parameter that has seen many large gradients accumulates a large sum, shrinking its effective learning rate. A parameter that has seen mostly small gradients accumulates a small sum, keeping its learning rate large.

This is particularly useful for sparse data — where some features appear rarely and others constantly. Rare features should be learned aggressively when they do appear (small accumulated gradient → large effective learning rate). Frequent features should be updated conservatively (large accumulated gradient → small effective learning rate).

Limitation: The sum of squared gradients only ever increases. Eventually every parameter's effective learning rate decays to near zero and learning stops, even if there is still meaningful gradient signal. This makes AdaGrad poorly suited to very long training runs.

RMSProp — Root Mean Square Propagation

RMSProp fixes AdaGrad's monotonically shrinking learning rate by replacing the accumulated sum of squared gradients with a moving average — only recent gradient history counts, not the entire history from the beginning of training.

Instead of the denominator growing without bound, old gradients gradually fade from the average (controlled by a decay factor, typically 0.9). The effective learning rate adapts based on recent gradient magnitudes, not all-time magnitudes.

This makes RMSProp well-suited to non-stationary problems — problems where the loss landscape changes during training, which describes most deep learning tasks. Empirically, RMSProp works particularly well for recurrent neural networks and was widely used before Adam became dominant.

Adam — Adaptive Moment Estimation

Adam combines the best ideas from both Momentum and RMSProp into a single optimizer, and is the default choice for the vast majority of deep learning tasks today.

It maintains two running averages for each parameter:

First moment (mean of gradients) — like Momentum, this tracks the direction and magnitude of recent gradients, providing the velocity that smooths oscillations and accelerates consistent directions.

Second moment (mean of squared gradients) — like RMSProp, this tracks recent gradient magnitudes, allowing the learning rate to adapt per-parameter based on how large recent gradients have been.

The update for each parameter divides the momentum term by the square root of the second moment — so parameters with large recent gradients take smaller steps, and parameters with small recent gradients take larger steps. Both effects happen simultaneously, per parameter, at every update step.

Adam also includes a bias correction step for the first few iterations, because the running averages are initialized at zero and would otherwise underestimate the true gradient moments at the start of training.

Why Adam is the default: It requires almost no tuning. The default hyperparameters (learning rate 0.001, decay rates 0.9 and 0.999) work well across an extraordinarily wide range of architectures and tasks. Momentum requires tuning the momentum coefficient. AdaGrad stops learning. RMSProp needs careful learning rate selection. Adam just works — which is why it is the starting point for nearly every new project.
AdamW is a common variant that separates the weight decay (regularization) term from the gradient update — the standard Adam implementation conflates the two, which can lead to suboptimal regularization. AdamW fixes this and is now preferred over Adam in transformer-based models (GPT, BERT, etc.).

Part 7

L1 and L2 Regularization

In one paragraph: L1 and L2 Regularization add a penalty term directly to your loss function — so instead of minimizing just $L$, you minimize $L + \lambda \cdot \text{penalty}(W)$. This changes the gradient equations you derived in a straightforward, traceable way. L2 (Ridge) pushes weights toward zero smoothly; L1 (Lasso) drives some weights to exactly zero, effectively removing those connections from the network entirely.

Every optimization technique in Part 6 addresses how the network learns — the mechanics of updating weights. Regularization addresses a different question entirely: what the network is learning, and whether it will generalize to data it has never seen.

A network that memorizes the training data perfectly but fails on new data is said to have overfit. Overfitting happens when the network learns to exploit specific quirks or noise in the training data rather than the underlying patterns. The symptom is a very low training loss paired with a high validation loss. Regularization is the family of techniques that penalize complexity to prevent this.

The Core Idea — Penalizing the Loss

Both L1 and L2 regularization work by modifying the loss function itself. Instead of minimizing just the prediction error $L$, we minimize a combined objective:

$$L_{\text{total}} = L + \lambda \cdot \text{penalty}(W)$$

where $\lambda$ (lambda) is the regularization strength — a hyperparameter you choose that controls how strongly the penalty is enforced. A larger $\lambda$ means the network cares more about keeping weights small and less about fitting the training data precisely.

The penalty term $\text{penalty}(W)$ is a measure of the complexity of the weights. The two choices — L1 and L2 — define this measure differently, and that difference in definition leads to a profound difference in behavior.

L2 Regularization — Ridge

L2 regularization defines the penalty as the sum of squared weights:

$$L_{\text{total}} = L + \frac{\lambda}{2} \sum_{i} w_i^2$$

The $\frac{1}{2}$ is a convenience factor — when we differentiate, it cancels the exponent of 2 cleanly, leaving just $\lambda w_i$ as the gradient contribution. This is the same trick used with the $\frac{1}{2}$ in MSE.

How It Changes the Gradient

Because the penalty is added directly to the loss, differentiating $L_{\text{total}}$ with respect to any weight $w_i$ simply adds the penalty's derivative to the original gradient:

$$\frac{\partial L_{\text{total}}}{\partial w_i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i} + \lambda w_i$$

The weight update rule then becomes:

$$w_i := w_i - \alpha \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i} + \lambda w_i\right) = w_i(1 - \alpha\lambda) - \alpha \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i}$$

The factor $(1 - \alpha\lambda)$ multiplies the current weight before the gradient step. Since $\alpha\lambda$ is a small positive number, this factor is slightly less than 1 — every update shrinks the weight by a small fraction before adding the gradient correction. This is why L2 regularization is also called weight decay — the weights decay toward zero at every step, regardless of the gradient.

What L2 produces: All weights are pushed smoothly and continuously toward zero, but none are forced all the way to exactly zero. The result is a network where many weights are small but present — no neuron is completely removed, but no single weight dominates. This tends to produce smooth, distributed representations that generalize well.

L1 Regularization — Lasso

L1 regularization defines the penalty as the sum of absolute values of weights:

$$L_{\text{total}} = L + \lambda \sum_{i} |w_i|$$

How It Changes the Gradient

The derivative of $|w_i|$ with respect to $w_i$ is $\text{sign}(w_i)$ — it is $+1$ when the weight is positive and $-1$ when the weight is negative (undefined at exactly zero, but this rarely occurs in practice):

$$\frac{\partial L_{\text{total}}}{\partial w_i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i} + \lambda \cdot \text{sign}(w_i)$$

The weight update becomes:

$$w_i := w_i - \alpha\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i} - \alpha\lambda \cdot \text{sign}(w_i)$$

The penalty subtracts a fixed constant $\alpha\lambda$ from positive weights and adds $\alpha\lambda$ to negative weights — pushing all weights toward zero by a constant amount at every step, regardless of their magnitude.

What L1 produces: Because the penalty is a fixed constant step rather than a proportional shrinkage, small weights get pushed all the way to exactly zero — a small weight cannot resist a constant push. Large weights can resist (their gradient contribution is larger than the penalty), but small weights cannot. The result is a sparse network — many weights become exactly zero, effectively removing those connections entirely. This is why L1 is used for feature selection: it automatically identifies which inputs are irrelevant and zeroes out their connections.

L1 vs L2 — The Key Difference

The fundamental distinction comes down to how the penalty grows as weights get larger.

For L2, the penalty grows as $w^2$ — quadratically. When a weight is large, the penalty is very large, creating strong pressure to reduce it. When a weight is small, the penalty is tiny, creating almost no pressure. L2 strongly penalizes outlier weights but barely touches weights already near zero — they are allowed to stay small rather than being pushed to exactly zero.

For L1, the penalty grows as $|w|$ — linearly. The pressure to reduce a weight is the same constant $\lambda$ regardless of whether the weight is large or tiny. This constant pressure eventually forces small weights to zero, which L2's diminishing pressure never achieves.

Choosing λ — The Regularization Strength

The regularization strength $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter that must be tuned. There is no universal right value — it depends on the dataset size, network size, and how much overfitting is observed.

The guiding principle is simple: $\lambda$ controls the tradeoff between fitting the training data and keeping weights small.

  • $\lambda$ too large — the penalty dominates, weights are forced near zero, and the network underfits. It cannot even learn the training data well.
  • $\lambda$ too small — the penalty is negligible, regularization has no effect, and the network overfits as if no regularization were applied.
  • $\lambda$ just right — the network fits the training data well while keeping weights from growing excessively large. Validated by monitoring the gap between training loss and validation loss.

In practice, $\lambda$ is found via cross-validation — train with several candidate values and select the one that produces the best validation performance. Common starting values are $10^{-4}$, $10^{-3}$, and $10^{-2}$.
