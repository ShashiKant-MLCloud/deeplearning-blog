1 · Network Architecture & Notation

We work with a 2-layer feedforward network: one hidden layer with 2 neurons, and one output layer with 1 neuron. Three input features feed in.

2 · Mathematical Foundations

Before the proof, we establish three tools that will be used throughout.

2.1 — Sigmoid Function & Its Derivative (Full Proof)

The sigmoid activation function is defined as:

$$\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$$

Its output is bounded in $(0, 1)$, making it useful for representing probabilities. But more importantly for backpropagation, its derivative has a beautiful closed form.

Proof of σ′(z)

Step 1 Rewrite as a power to enable the chain rule:

$$\sigma(z) = (1 + e^{-z})^{-1}$$

Step 2 Let $u = 1 + e^{-z}$, so $\sigma = u^{-1}$. Apply chain rule:

$$\frac{d\sigma}{dz} = \frac{d\sigma}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dz}$$

Step 3 Compute each factor:

$$\frac{d\sigma}{du} = -u^{-2} = \frac{-1}{(1 + e^{-z})^2}$$ $$\frac{du}{dz} = \frac{d}{dz}(1 + e^{-z}) = -e^{-z}$$

Step 4 Multiply them together:

$$\frac{d\sigma}{dz} = \frac{-1}{(1+e^{-z})^2} \cdot (-e^{-z}) = \frac{e^{-z}}{(1+e^{-z})^2}$$

Step 5 Factor into a self-referential form. Add and subtract 1 in the numerator:

$$= \frac{(1 + e^{-z}) - 1}{(1+e^{-z})^2} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} - \frac{1}{(1+e^{-z})^2} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}\left(1 - \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}\right)$$
$$\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)\cdot(1 - \sigma(z)) = A \cdot (1 - A)$$
Why this matters: The derivative of sigmoid is expressed entirely in terms of sigmoid itself. Since we already compute $\sigma(z)$ in the forward pass, we reuse it for free during backpropagation — no extra computation needed. This is computational elegance.

2.2 — The Chain Rule of Calculus

For a composed function where $L$ depends on $A$, $A$ depends on $Z$, and $Z$ depends on $W$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial A} \cdot \frac{\partial A}{\partial Z} \cdot \frac{\partial Z}{\partial W}$$

This is the engine of backpropagation. Every gradient we compute is an application of this rule, sometimes nested across multiple layers.

2.3 — MSE Loss Function

We use Mean Squared Error with a convenient $\frac{1}{2}$ factor:

$$L = \frac{1}{2}(y - \hat{y})^2$$

The $\frac{1}{2}$ is purely for algebraic convenience — when we differentiate, the exponent 2 cancels it, giving cleaner gradients. It does not change the location of the minimum.

Computing ∂L/∂ŷ — and why the ordering doesn't matter

A natural question arises: does writing the loss as $(y - \hat{y})^2$ versus $(\hat{y} - y)^2$ change the math? The answer is no — both forms produce identical gradients.

📎 Proof: Both orderings give the same gradient

Case 1: $L = \frac{1}{2}(y - \hat{y})^2$. Let $u = (y - \hat{y})$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2u \cdot \frac{\partial u}{\partial \hat{y}}$$

Now, $u = y - \hat{y}$ where $y$ is a constant (a fixed training label). So $\frac{\partial u}{\partial \hat{y}} = \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{y}}[y] - \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{y}}[\hat{y}] = 0 - 1 = -1$.

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = u \cdot (-1) = -(y - \hat{y}) = (\hat{y} - y)$$

Case 2: $L = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{y} - y)^2$. Let $u = (\hat{y} - y)$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2u \cdot \frac{\partial u}{\partial \hat{y}} = u \cdot (+1) = (\hat{y} - y)$$

Both give $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = (\hat{y} - y)$. This is mathematically guaranteed because $(y-\hat{y})^2 = (\hat{y}-y)^2$ — squaring eliminates any sign difference.

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = (\hat{y} - y)$$
Why the sign is (ŷ − y) and not (y − ŷ): This sign is critical for gradient descent to work correctly. If $\hat{y} = 0.9$ and $y = 1$, the gradient is $-0.1$ (negative), and gradient descent subtracts a negative value — pushing $\hat{y}$ up toward $1$. If the sign were flipped, the network would learn in reverse.

3 · Forward Propagation

Forward propagation is the process of flowing inputs through the network to produce a prediction. Each layer performs two operations: a weighted sum (pre-activation), then an activation function.

Layer 1: Input → Hidden

Pre-activation (weighted sum):

For each hidden neuron $j \in \{1, 2\}$:

$$z^1_j = \sum_{i=1}^{3} w^1_{ji} \cdot x_i + b^1_j$$ Expanded explicitly: $$z^1_1 = w^1_{11}x_1 + w^1_{12}x_2 + w^1_{13}x_3 + b^1_1$$ $$z^1_2 = w^1_{21}x_1 + w^1_{22}x_2 + w^1_{23}x_3 + b^1_2$$ In matrix form: $$\mathbf{Z^1} = \mathbf{W^1} \cdot \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{b^1}$$ $$\begin{bmatrix} z^1_1 \\ z^1_2 \end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} w^1_{11} & w^1_{12} & w^1_{13} \\ w^1_{21} & w^1_{22} & w^1_{23} \end{bmatrix}}_{(2\times3)} \cdot \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}}_{(3\times1)} + \begin{bmatrix} b^1_1 \\ b^1_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Dimension check: $(2\times3)\cdot(3\times1) + (2\times1) = (2\times1)$ ✅

Post-activation (apply sigmoid):

$$\mathbf{A^1} = \sigma(\mathbf{Z^1}) = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma(z^1_1) \\ \sigma(z^1_2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a^1_1 \\ a^1_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Layer 2: Hidden → Output

Pre-activation:

$$Z^2 = w^2_{11} \cdot a^1_1 + w^2_{21} \cdot a^1_2 + b^2$$ In matrix form: $$Z^2 = \mathbf{W^2} \cdot \mathbf{A^1} + b^2$$ $$Z^2 = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} w^2_{11} & w^2_{21} \end{bmatrix}}_{(1\times2)} \cdot \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a^1_1 \\ a^1_2 \end{bmatrix}}_{(2\times1)} + b^2$$

Dimension check: $(1\times2)\cdot(2\times1) = (1\times1)$ = scalar ✅

Post-activation:

$$\hat{y} = A^2 = \sigma(Z^2) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-Z^2}}$$

Compute Loss

$$\boxed{L = \frac{1}{2}(y - \hat{y})^2}$$

This single scalar tells us how far our prediction is from the truth. The entire goal of training is to minimize this number.

Forward Pass Summary:
  X  ──▶  Z¹ = W¹X + b¹  ──▶  A¹ = σ(Z¹)  ──▶  Z² = W²A¹ + b²  ──▶  ŷ = σ(Z²)

4 · Backpropagation — The Full Proof

Backpropagation computes the gradient of the loss with respect to every weight in the network by applying the chain rule systematically — working backwards from the output to the input. The dependency chain is:

L  →  A²  →  Z²  →  W², b²
                  →  A¹  →  Z¹  →  W¹, b¹

4.1 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. A² (Output Activation)

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial A^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial A^2}\left[\frac{1}{2}(y - A^2)^2\right] = -(y - A^2) = (\hat{y} - y)$$

We name this the output error signal:

$$\delta^2_A = \frac{\partial L}{\partial A^2} = (\hat{y} - y)$$

4.2 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Z² (Output Pre-Activation)

Since $A^2 = \sigma(Z^2)$, by chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial Z^2} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial A^2} \cdot \frac{\partial A^2}{\partial Z^2} = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot \sigma'(Z^2)$$

Using our proven sigmoid derivative $\sigma'(Z^2) = A^2(1 - A^2) = \hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$:

$$\delta^2 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial Z^2} = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot \hat{y}(1 - \hat{y})$$
Vanishing gradient in action: When $\hat{y}$ is very close to 0 or 1, $\hat{y}(1-\hat{y}) \approx 0$, making $\delta^2 \approx 0$. The network barely updates — it's so confident that it barely learns. This is the vanishing gradient problem inherent to sigmoid.

4.3 — Gradients for W² and b²

Why ∂Z²/∂w²_{k1} = a¹_k — Detailed Proof

Write $Z^2$ fully for our 2-hidden-neuron network:

$$Z^2 = w^2_{11} \cdot a^1_1 + w^2_{21} \cdot a^1_2 + b^2$$

This is a simple linear equation — a weighted sum. When we differentiate with respect to a specific weight, say $w^2_{11}$, every other term treats that weight as a constant and vanishes:

$$\frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial w^2_{11}} = \frac{\partial}{\partial w^2_{11}}\left[w^2_{11} \cdot a^1_1 + w^2_{21} \cdot a^1_2 + b^2\right]$$ $$= a^1_1 + 0 + 0 = a^1_1$$

Similarly for $w^2_{21}$:

$$\frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial w^2_{21}} = 0 + a^1_2 + 0 = a^1_2$$
💡 Analogy

Think of $f = 3p + 7q + 2r$. Then $\partial f/\partial p = 3$, $\partial f/\partial q = 7$, $\partial f/\partial r = 2$. The derivative w.r.t. any one variable returns its coefficient — everything else vanishes. In our case, the "coefficient" of $w^2_{k1}$ is $a^1_k$.

Generalizing: for any weight $w^2_{k1}$ where $k \in \{1, 2\}$:

$$\frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial w^2_{k1}} = a^1_k$$
Deep meaning: The gradient for each weight equals the activation that flowed through it. Weights connected to highly active neurons receive larger updates — the network naturally focuses learning where activations are strong.

Applying the Chain Rule for W²

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w^2_{k1}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial Z^2} \cdot \frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial w^2_{k1}} = \delta^2 \cdot a^1_k$$ In full matrix form:
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^2}} = \delta^2 \cdot (\mathbf{A^1})^T = \delta^2 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} a^1_1 & a^1_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Dimension check: scalar $\cdot$ $(1\times2)$ = $(1\times2)$ — same shape as $\mathbf{W^2}$ ✅

Gradient for b²

Since $\frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial b^2} = 1$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial b^2} = \delta^2$$

4.4 — Propagating Error Back to the Hidden Layer

Why (W²)ᵀ Appears — It's Not Arbitrary

We need $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}$ — how much does the loss depend on each hidden neuron's output?

The naive scalar chain rule would suggest multiplying $\delta^2$ by $W^2$. But as soon as variables become vectors, the scalar chain rule upgrades to the matrix chain rule, which naturally introduces the transpose of the Jacobian:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}} = \left(\frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}\right)^T \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial Z^2}$$

Since $Z^2 = \mathbf{W^2} \cdot \mathbf{A^1} + b^2$, the Jacobian $\frac{\partial Z^2}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}$ has shape $(1\times2)$ (one output, two inputs), and equals $\mathbf{W^2}$ exactly. Taking its transpose:

📐 Dimension Proof — Why the transpose is mandatory

Our goal: $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}$ must have shape $(2\times1)$ — one gradient per hidden neuron.

Without transpose: $W^2 \cdot \delta^2 = (1\times2)\cdot(1\times1)$ → ❌ inner dimensions 2 and 1 don't match

Swapped order: $\delta^2 \cdot W^2 = (1\times1)\cdot(1\times2) = (1\times2)$ → valid but wrong shape (we need $(2\times1)$, not $(1\times2)$)

With transpose: $(W^2)^T \cdot \delta^2 = (2\times1)\cdot(1\times1) = (2\times1)$ → ✅ valid AND correct shape

The transpose is demanded by the matrix chain rule — it is not inserted to "fix" dimensions after the fact. The scalar chain rule assumes scalars; the matrix chain rule for a scalar $L$ depending on vector $\mathbf{A}$ through vector $\mathbf{Z}$ is always $\mathbf{J}^T \cdot \nabla_Z L$, where $\mathbf{J}$ is the Jacobian. The transpose is baked in by definition.

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}} = (\mathbf{W^2})^T \cdot \delta^2 = \begin{bmatrix} w^2_{11} \\ w^2_{21} \end{bmatrix} \cdot \delta^2 = \begin{bmatrix} w^2_{11} \cdot \delta^2 \\ w^2_{21} \cdot \delta^2 \end{bmatrix}$$
Forward vs Backward — same weights, reversed flow:
Forward: $W^2$ compresses (2 hidden neurons → 1 output). Backward: $(W^2)^T$ expands (1 error signal → 2 hidden gradients). The same weight magnitudes that controlled forward influence now control backward blame. A weight of 0.8 that strongly drove the output in the forward pass will strongly carry the error signal back in the backward pass.
DirectionOperationShape Flow

Backward pass$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}} = (\mathbf{W^2})^T \cdot \delta^2$$(2\times1)\cdot(1\times1) \to (2\times1)$

4.5 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Hidden Pre-Activation (Z¹)

This is the central goal of the hidden layer backpropagation — computing $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}$, also written as $\delta^1$. Once we have this, deriving the gradients for $\mathbf{W^1}$ and $\mathbf{b^1}$ becomes straightforward.

The full dependency chain from $L$ back to $\mathbf{Z^1}$ passes through two intermediate quantities we must account for:

L  ──▶  Z²  ──▶  A¹  ──▶  Z¹

Chain rule: ∂L/∂Z¹ = (∂A¹/∂Z¹)ᵀ · ∂L/∂A¹

Expanding each factor via the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}\right)^T \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}}_{\text{computed in 4.4} = (\mathbf{W^2})^T \cdot \delta^2}$$

We already have $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}} = (\mathbf{W^2})^T \cdot \delta^2$ from Section 4.4. The remaining piece we need is $\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}$ — the Jacobian of the sigmoid activation at the hidden layer.

4.5.1 — The Jacobian of A¹ w.r.t. Z¹

Since both $\mathbf{A^1}$ and $\mathbf{Z^1}$ are vectors of shape (2×1), the derivative $\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}$ is a Jacobian matrix — a structured table of all possible partial derivatives between every output element and every input element.

How the Jacobian is Formed

We have 2 outputs ($a^1_1, a^1_2$) and 2 inputs ($z^1_1, z^1_2$), giving $2\times2 = 4$ possible combinations:

            ← differentiate w.r.t. each z →

               z¹₁                 z¹₂

a¹₁  |   ∂a¹₁/∂z¹₁          ∂a¹₁/∂z¹₂   |

a¹₂  |   ∂a¹₂/∂z¹₁          ∂a¹₂/∂z¹₂   |

The rule: Rows = outputs (which A element), Columns = inputs (which Z element). Like a multiplication table, it exhaustively covers every combination.

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial a^1_1}{\partial z^1_1} & \frac{\partial a^1_1}{\partial z^1_2} \\ \frac{\partial a^1_2}{\partial z^1_1} & \frac{\partial a^1_2}{\partial z^1_2} \end{bmatrix}$$

Computing Every Element

First, recall from the forward pass:

$$a^1_1 = \sigma(z^1_1) \quad\quad a^1_2 = \sigma(z^1_2)$$

Each neuron's activation depends only on its own pre-activation. They do not mix.

Top-Left: $\partial a^1_1/\partial z^1_1$ — differentiating neuron 1's activation w.r.t. its own input:

$$\frac{\partial a^1_1}{\partial z^1_1} = \sigma'(z^1_1) = a^1_1(1 - a^1_1) \quad \checkmark \text{ non-zero}$$

Top-Right: $\partial a^1_1/\partial z^1_2$ — differentiating neuron 1's activation w.r.t. neuron 2's input:

$$a^1_1 = \sigma(z^1_1) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z^1_1}}$$

There is no $z^1_2$ anywhere in this expression. So $a^1_1$ is a constant with respect to $z^1_2$:

$$\frac{\partial a^1_1}{\partial z^1_2} = 0 \quad \text{(different neurons are independent)}$$

Bottom-Left: $\partial a^1_2/\partial z^1_1$ — same reasoning as top-right, just reversed:

$$\frac{\partial a^1_2}{\partial z^1_1} = 0$$

Bottom-Right: $\partial a^1_2/\partial z^1_2$ — neuron 2's activation w.r.t. its own input:

$$\frac{\partial a^1_2}{\partial z^1_2} = a^1_2(1 - a^1_2) \quad \checkmark \text{ non-zero}$$

The Result: A Diagonal Jacobian

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \begin{bmatrix} a^1_1(1-a^1_1) & 0 \\ 0 & a^1_2(1-a^1_2) \end{bmatrix}$$
PositionValueWhy
Diagonal ($i = j$)$a^1_j(1-a^1_j)$Neuron differentiates w.r.t. its OWN input — non-zero
Off-diagonal ($i \neq j$)$0$Neurons are independent — other neuron's $z$ never appears
Why neurons are independent at this stage: Each hidden neuron computes its own $z^1_j = \sum_i w^1_{ji}x_i + b^1_j$ using its own weights. The $z$ values are never mixed within the same layer — they only interact in the next layer. This independence is what collapses the full Jacobian into a diagonal, which in turn is why backprop uses element-wise multiplication (⊙) rather than full matrix multiplication.

4.6 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Hidden Pre-Activation (Z¹) — Computing δ¹

We now have all the pieces needed. The complete chain rule flow for $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}$ is:

∂L/∂Z¹  =  (∂A¹/∂Z¹)ᵀ  ·  ∂L/∂A¹
         =  (∂A¹/∂Z¹)ᵀ  ·  (W²)ᵀ · δ²
         =  Diagonal Jacobian  ·  Back-propagated error

Substituting the three known quantities:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}\right)^T}_{\text{Diagonal Jacobian from 4.5.1}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}}_{(\mathbf{W^2})^T \cdot \delta^2 \text{ from 4.4}}$$

4.6.1 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Hidden Pre-Activation (Z¹) — Full Expansion

Substituting the diagonal Jacobian from Section 4.5.1 and $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{A^1}}$ from Section 4.4:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \begin{bmatrix} a^1_1(1-a^1_1) & 0 \\ 0 & a^1_2(1-a^1_2) \end{bmatrix}^T \cdot \begin{bmatrix} w^2_{11}\cdot\delta^2 \\ w^2_{21}\cdot\delta^2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Since the diagonal Jacobian is symmetric (diagonal matrices equal their own transpose), $\mathbf{J}^T = \mathbf{J}$, so:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \begin{bmatrix} a^1_1(1-a^1_1) & 0 \\ 0 & a^1_2(1-a^1_2) \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} w^2_{11}\cdot\delta^2 \\ w^2_{21}\cdot\delta^2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Carrying out the matrix-vector multiplication row by row:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z^1_1} = a^1_1(1-a^1_1) \cdot w^2_{11}\cdot\delta^2 + 0 \cdot w^2_{21}\cdot\delta^2 = a^1_1(1-a^1_1) \cdot w^2_{11}\cdot\delta^2$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z^1_2} = 0 \cdot w^2_{11}\cdot\delta^2 + a^1_2(1-a^1_2) \cdot w^2_{21}\cdot\delta^2 = a^1_2(1-a^1_2) \cdot w^2_{21}\cdot\delta^2$$

Since multiplying a diagonal matrix by a vector just scales each element independently, this is identical to element-wise (⊙) multiplication. We name this result $\delta^1$:

$$\delta^1 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} = \left[(\mathbf{W^2})^T \cdot \delta^2\right] \odot \mathbf{A^1}(1 - \mathbf{A^1})$$

Written out element by element:

$$\delta^1_1 = w^2_{11} \cdot \delta^2 \cdot a^1_1(1-a^1_1)$$ $$\delta^1_2 = w^2_{21} \cdot \delta^2 \cdot a^1_2(1-a^1_2)$$
Reading the formula: Each hidden neuron's error signal $\delta^1_j$ is the product of three things — (1) how strongly its output influenced the next layer via $w^2_{j1}$, (2) the output layer's error $\delta^2$, and (3) how sensitive its own activation was to its input $a^1_j(1-a^1_j)$. All three factors must be non-zero for learning to happen at this neuron.

4.7 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Hidden Weights (W¹)

With $\delta^1 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}$ now fully computed, we apply the chain rule one final step. Since $\mathbf{Z^1} = \mathbf{W^1} \cdot \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{b^1}$, differentiating with respect to $\mathbf{W^1}$ follows the same logic as Section 4.3 — the derivative of a weighted sum with respect to one weight returns the input that was multiplied by it. The chain rule gives:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} = \delta^1 \cdot \mathbf{X}^T$$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} = \delta^1 \cdot \mathbf{X}^T$$

Expanded in full matrix form:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} = \begin{bmatrix} \delta^1_1 \\ \delta^1_2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 & x_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \delta^1_1 x_1 & \delta^1_1 x_2 & \delta^1_1 x_3 \\ \delta^1_2 x_1 & \delta^1_2 x_2 & \delta^1_2 x_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Dimension check: $(2\times1)\cdot(1\times3) = (2\times3)$ — same shape as $\mathbf{W^1}$ ✅

Same pattern as W²: Each element of the gradient matrix is the product of the error signal at that neuron ($\delta^1_j$) and the input that flowed through the corresponding weight ($x_i$). The network assigns more blame to weights that carried larger inputs.

4.8 — Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Hidden Bias (b¹)

Since $\mathbf{Z^1} = \mathbf{W^1} \cdot \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{b^1}$, differentiating with respect to $\mathbf{b^1}$ is straightforward — the bias enters $\mathbf{Z^1}$ additively with a coefficient of 1. By the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{b^1}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{Z^1}}{\partial \mathbf{b^1}} = \delta^1 \cdot 1$$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{b^1}} = \delta^1 = \begin{bmatrix} \delta^1_1 \\ \delta^1_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Dimension check: $(2\times1)$ — same shape as $\mathbf{b^1}$ ✅

Why the bias gradient is just δ¹: The bias does not scale with any input — it is always added with weight 1. So its partial derivative contribution is always 1, and the full gradient reduces to purely the error signal. This is consistent with how the output bias $b^2$ gradient reduced to $\delta^2$ in Section 4.3.

5 · Weight Update Equations

With all gradients computed, gradient descent updates every parameter by stepping in the direction that reduces the loss:

$$\mathbf{W^2} := \mathbf{W^2} - \alpha \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^2}} = \mathbf{W^2} - \alpha \cdot \delta^2 \cdot (\mathbf{A^1})^T$$ $$b^2 := b^2 - \alpha \cdot \delta^2$$ $$\mathbf{W^1} := \mathbf{W^1} - \alpha \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} = \mathbf{W^1} - \alpha \cdot \delta^1 \cdot \mathbf{X}^T$$ $$\mathbf{b^1} := \mathbf{b^1} - \alpha \cdot \delta^1$$
The subtraction is intentional: The gradient points in the direction of steepest increase in loss. By subtracting, we step toward the minimum. The learning rate $\alpha$ controls how large a step we take — too large and we overshoot; too small and convergence is painfully slow.

6 · Complete Numerical Example

Setup

Inputs:       X  = [0.5, 0.3, 0.2]ᵀ
True label:   y  = 1
Learning rate: α = 0.5

W¹ = [[ 0.4, -0.2,  0.3],    shape (2×3)
      [ 0.1,  0.5, -0.4]]

b¹ = [0.1, -0.1]ᵀ

W² = [[0.6, -0.3]]            shape (1×2)   ← w²₁₁=0.6, w²₂₁=-0.3

b²  = 0.2

Step 1 — Forward Pass: Layer 1

$$z^1_1 = (0.4)(0.5) + (-0.2)(0.3) + (0.3)(0.2) + 0.1 = 0.20 - 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.10 = 0.30$$ $$z^1_2 = (0.1)(0.5) + (0.5)(0.3) + (-0.4)(0.2) + (-0.1) = 0.05 + 0.15 - 0.08 - 0.10 = 0.02$$ $$a^1_1 = \sigma(0.30) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-0.30}} = \frac{1}{1+0.7408} \approx 0.5744$$ $$a^1_2 = \sigma(0.02) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-0.02}} = \frac{1}{1+0.9802} \approx 0.5050$$

Step 2 — Forward Pass: Layer 2

$$Z^2 = (0.6)(0.5744) + (-0.3)(0.5050) + 0.2 = 0.3446 - 0.1515 + 0.2 = 0.3931$$ $$\hat{y} = \sigma(0.3931) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-0.3931}} \approx 0.5972$$

Step 3 — Compute Loss

$$L = \frac{1}{2}(1 - 0.5972)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.4028)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.1622) = 0.0811$$

Step 4 — Backprop: Output Layer δ²

$$\delta^2 = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot \hat{y}(1-\hat{y}) = (0.5972-1)\cdot0.5972\cdot0.4028 = (-0.4028)(0.2405) = -0.0969$$

Step 5 — Gradients for W² and b²

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w^2_{11}} = \delta^2 \cdot a^1_1 = (-0.0969)(0.5744) = -0.0557$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w^2_{21}} = \delta^2 \cdot a^1_2 = (-0.0969)(0.5050) = -0.0489$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial b^2} = \delta^2 = -0.0969$$

Step 6 — Backprop: Hidden Layer δ¹

First, propagate error back through W²:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial a^1_1} = w^2_{11} \cdot \delta^2 = (0.6)(-0.0969) = -0.0581$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial a^1_2} = w^2_{21} \cdot \delta^2 = (-0.3)(-0.0969) = +0.0291$$

Then multiply by sigmoid derivative of each hidden neuron:

$$\delta^1_1 = (-0.0581) \cdot a^1_1(1-a^1_1) = (-0.0581)(0.5744)(0.4256) = -0.01421$$ $$\delta^1_2 = (0.0291) \cdot a^1_2(1-a^1_2) = (0.0291)(0.5050)(0.4950) = 0.00727$$

Step 7 — Gradients for W¹

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W^1}} = \begin{bmatrix} -0.01421 \\ 0.00727 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 & 0.3 & 0.2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -0.00711 & -0.00426 & -0.00284 \\ 0.00364 & 0.00218 & 0.00145 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 8 — Update All Weights (α = 0.5)

Output layer:
  w²₁₁ := 0.6  - 0.5×(-0.0557) =  0.6279
  w²₂₁ := -0.3 - 0.5×(-0.0489) = -0.2756
  b²   := 0.2  - 0.5×(-0.0969) =  0.2485

Hidden layer (first row of W¹):
  w¹₁₁ := 0.4  - 0.5×(-0.00711) =  0.4036
  w¹₁₂ := -0.2 - 0.5×(-0.00426) = -0.1979
  w¹₁₃ := 0.3  - 0.5×(-0.00284) =  0.3014

Step 9 — Verify Progress

Running a new forward pass with the updated weights produces a $\hat{y}$ slightly larger than 0.5972 — closer to our target of 1.0. Repeating this process thousands of times drives the loss toward zero and the prediction toward the true label.

7 · Formula Reference Sheet

Forward Propagation
Z¹ = W¹ · X + b¹
A¹ = σ(Z¹)
Z² = W² · A¹ + b²
ŷ = A² = σ(Z²)
L = ½(y − ŷ)²
Sigmoid & Derivative
σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ᶻ)
σ′(z) = σ(z)·(1 − σ(z))
∂L/∂ŷ = (ŷ − y)
Backward Propagation — Output Layer
δ² = (ŷ − y) · ŷ(1 − ŷ)
∂L/∂W² = δ² · (A¹)ᵀ ← shape: (1×2)
∂L/∂b² = δ²
Backward Propagation — Hidden Layer
∂L/∂A¹ = (W²)ᵀ · δ² ← matrix chain rule
δ¹ = [(W²)ᵀ · δ²] ⊙ A¹(1−A¹) ← ⊙ = element-wise
∂L/∂W¹ = δ¹ · Xᵀ ← shape: (2×3)
∂L/∂b¹ = δ¹
Weight Updates
W² := W² − α · ∂L/∂W²
b² := b² − α · ∂L/∂b²
W¹ := W¹ − α · ∂L/∂W¹
b¹ := b¹ − α · ∂L/∂b¹

The Mathematical Soul of a Neural Network

Strip everything away and what remains is a single composed function:

$$L = \frac{1}{2}\left(y - \sigma\left(\mathbf{W^2}\cdot\sigma\left(\mathbf{W^1}\mathbf{X}+\mathbf{b^1}\right)+b^2\right)\right)^2$$

Backpropagation is systematic differentiation of this composition — layer by layer, outside to inside — using the chain rule. The sigmoid's self-referential derivative makes it computationally efficient, the matrix chain rule naturally produces the transpose, and the diagonal Jacobian collapses to simple element-wise multiplication.

Everything else — the architecture, the notation, the numerical example — is just making sure we apply these three ideas correctly, at the right dimensions, in the right order.
